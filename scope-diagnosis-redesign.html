Redesigning the self triage process in Check Legal Aid

The project

TThe CLA team have been working on the scope diagnosis or self triage part of the Check Legal Aid journey.

It is a complex state of user journeys that combine emotional user states and legal / policy driven terminology.

This project is still ongoing but has been created to improve the experience for users when checking if they are eligible for legal aid and attempting to match their situation to the categories of law.

This project is broken down into two parts:

  1. Component design challenge
  2. User journey challenge

My Role

My role in this project has been interchangeable since I joined the MoJ. I initially started the project looking at interactions at a compoment design level where I was tasked with improving the accessibility of the scope option component.

I also supported our user researcher in several research sessions when the project began to look at understanding how users navigate and interpret the information they see. In any true discover, the role of the designer is to observe and support and this is what I did.

I also managed a cabinet office fast streamer who I supported, working on a spike of the diagnosis journey based on the initial round of user testing.

We are about to (as of Nov 2020) begin to look at design ideation based on a large amount of research work.

The Team

There are so many facets to this piece of work and it truly requires a great amount of effort from the delivery team. I am currently working on this project with a:

  • User researcher (only available on the scope part of the work)
  • Business analyst
  • Content designer
  • Front end developer
  • Previously Cabinet Office fast streamer

The problem

Although the satisfaction rate of our service is in the mid 70 percentile, users have reported that self diagnosing their issue is difficult and complex.

For the LAA to be able to provide a quality of service to our users, and a majority of the time in vulnerable states of wellbeing, it is critical for us to continue to improve the standard of our service.

This is even more important because it is generally the first part of the process that a user will see on their journey to get legal aid and is paired directly back to our product road map.

In addition to this, the existing components used on the CLA journey were outdated and failed to comply with accessibility requirements. With the new accessibility legislation coming into effect on 23rd September 2020, this needed to be addressed quickly.

1. Scope Diagnosis Component

My Approach

Problem statementThe grey boxes where users select scope are no longer used across government. They also fail accessibility standards. We need to bring them to GOV.UK standards.

Working on the scope diagnosis compoment, I needed to understand if their was a component across gov that could be utlitised to quickly improve the design and accessibility of our existing component.

I reached out on the cross government slack to gather information from the design community and discovered that our service was using an old gov elements design that had never made it into the design system.

I also received some insightful feedback about using alternative components such as radio buttons or navigation links.

I then took these sketches and moved directly to the prototype kit as the suggestions from across Gov already existed in the design system. I also created an updated design iteration of the existing scope diagnosis interaction.

I utilised the MoJ design community and delivery team to get feedback on the design concepts and also carried out some responsive testing myself.

This informed the decision making process and allowed the team to quickly move the selected design option of the existing button as it added more immediate value for less effort into staging.

I also presented our design solution to the Design System meeting which gained positive feedback from the Product Manager and Head of Interaction Design at GDS.

Outcomes

For the business

The outcomes for the business of this component work were quite simple. I created an improved iteration of the design that tested well with accessibility tools such as screen readers. It also performed well during a DAC audit of our service.

This meant that their were no issues for the business around accessibility.

For the user

The main benefit for our users was that we have created an improved accessible design that is:

  • Clearer in presentation
  • Has improved colour contrast

2. Improving the user journey through self diagnosis

My Approach

This part of the project required a more active listening approach than conceptualising a design, but led to two outputs that would help continue to drive the project forward. These were:

  1. I supported our user researcher with several workshops with the team to understand the research findings and how they would be applied to the existing service.
  2. I mapped out all of the existing user journeys for each category of law and facilitated a workshop with the team to gather all of our understanding and issues with the flows and content.

These workshops were run in both a physical but also remote setting utilising post it's and Miro boards.

I documented the workshop notes for the team. From these sessions we had a better understanding of the challenges that our users faced but it led to us asking more questions about our service and how we could improve it for the users.

Working with the content designer, I created several design iterations which myself and our BA presented back to our stakeholders over several conference calls to take them through design decisions and listen to their feedback.

After several rounds iterations and sign off from our stakeholders the design work was handed over to our developers to implement the new flows and components on the staging platform.

These developmental changes were tested by the team and also certain stakeholders to ensure that before moving into production they were meeting the user needs and also consistent with the designs.

I'm addition to this, I presented our work to the wider Justice Services community to share working and promote the fantastic work that the team had carried out on behalf of our stakeholders.

Outcomes

Outcomes for the user

The key outcome for the user was two fold.

Firstly these changes were gratefully met by the contract manager who presented our work to the service owner.

Operators were able to clearly identify when users were going through a specific part of the journey by allowing them to easily choose the correct option on the interface.

They were also clearly alerted to supporting information through the use of a new alert component that informed their decision making whilst on the phone to users.

Outcomes for the business

SThis work provided a rapid , critical change to the service that helped reduce any long term problems for the public, operators and specialist providers using the service.

We received great feedback from the contract manager on our work with them informing us that the design presentations made it very clear for them to understand the processes.
